Overview

Cal State LA’s Special Collections and Archives (SCA) is a University repository committed to providing access to primary source research materials for the Cal State LA academic community, scholars and the research community at large. According to their mission statement, SCA is committed to capturing the “diverse lives and historical narratives reflected in our heavily first-generation student body, and the major contributions their communities have made to the dynamic culture of Southern California” (California State University, Los Angeles, 2026).

The scope of SCA’s collections policy includes; manuscripts collections, university archives, rare books, audiovisual materials and digital materials. Some of the collections included in the repository are the East LA Archives, Mesoamerica and Colonial Mexico Manuscript Collection and Rare Book Collection, and Central America Memoria Historica Archive.

Azalea Camacho is the Head of Special Collections and Archives at Cal State LA, where she has worked for the last 12 years. Azalea graduated with her MLIS with a specialization in Archival Studies from San Jose State University and her early experience includes a digitization project for JSTOR through UCLA at the Southern Regional Library. In 2014, she began her journey at Cal State LA as a University Librarian Archivist, later becoming a Special Collections Librarian and eventually assuming the Head position in the archives. Azalea also serves as the Vice President for the Society of California Archivists and is a member of the REFORMA Archive housed at Cal State LA’s SCA.

During our interview with Azalea, she revealed that Cal State LA has placed a major emphasis on AI integration into the library and classroom sphere, but has yet to come up with any regulations or progress plan. This task has fallen onto the faculty and students to navigate themselves. Thus far, professors have been required to disclose their AI policy clearly in their syllabi, but Azalea would like to push for training on responsible AI use and AI literacy. Azalea asserts that while the field is trending towards digitization and ways AI can be integrated, soft skills such as communication are far more paramount for succeeding in the archival field.

Throughout her career, Azalea has worked with several digital asset management systems, including CONTENTdm, DSpace, and Luna. She recommends internships that provide experience with these systems, along with training in archival description and preservation metadata. Despite the importance of technical skills, she stresses the value of being able to navigate social situations and being capable of outreach with donors and community members.

She shares that AI has not significantly altered SCA’s core operations but has assisted with tasks such as metadata creation and transcription, using tools like OtterAI for oral histories. Human oversight remains necessary to ensure accuracy. The broader AI initiative within the CSU system has also created additional labor expectations for librarians, who must now develop guidelines, lead workshops, and educate the campus community about AI without prior training or consultation.

Typical Tasks

At Cal State LA’s SCA, Azalea oversees all of the operations such as, archival processing, instruction, reference, public services, outreach, collection development, maintenance and management of the student workers. Azalea is currently the only full-time faculty in Special Collections but oversees contracted, temporary archivists and student archivists. Along with the day-to-day management of the students, Azalea has also implemented a mentorship program, called the Student Archivist Program. This program includes hands-on training in the archive in physical processing, metadata creation and reference services, one-on-one meetings with each student to explore professional goals and development, and support for personal projects (research, exhibition or teaching).

Considering Azalea is the only full-time faculty in Special Collections, her responsibilities cover almost all aspects of the archive, from processing to management. However, this level of responsibility is in large part due to the shrinking size of the library budget at Cal State LA. For example, Cal State Northridge’s Special Collections employs five full-time archivists, a special collections librarian, a reading room supervisor and a head of the department. An aspiring Head of a University Special Collections and Archive is more likely to find relevant experience resembling the managerial tasks of CSUN’s Head rather than in Azalea’s position.

Professional Context

Azalea graduated with her MLIS with a specialization in Archival Studies from San Jose State University. All available job postings for the title of Head of Special Collections listed an MLIS as a requirement, however, in our interview, Azalea mentioned how the previous Director of the SCA held a PhD, not an MLIS. A job posting for the Director of Middlebury’s Special Collections and Archives requires an MLIS as well as “five to seven years of professional experience in special collections including a minimum of three years of management, supervision, and mentoring staff” (Middlebury College, 2026).

Azalea recommended checking archivesgig.com for job postings and signing up for the Los Angeles Archivists’ Collective newsletter to keep informed about resources and jobs. Azalea is currently the Vice President for the Society of California Archivists, which is “a professional association devoted to the support and development of individuals and institutions that collect, care for, and provide access to the documentary heritage of California and adjoining areas.” She is also a member of REFORMA National Archives, an association dedicated in service of uplifting Latinx and Spanish-speaking information professionals and the needs of the Latinx and Spanish-speaking communities. The REFORMA National Archives are held and maintained at Cal State LA’s SCA. They hold yearly conferences around the country as the “premier training and networking event for those dedicated to library services for Latinos and Spanish-speaking communities.” REFORMA also publishes their own newsletter on topics and issues surrounding these communities.

Job Market Outlook

The job market for archivists is projected to grow 6% between 2024–2034. Hybrid roles have increased opportunities.

This reflects the growth that special collections specifically experienced the decade before. On the Rare Books and Manuscripts news blog, advertised special collections positions rose from 27 in 2014 to 121 in 2016, then to 140 in 2019.

While hiring slowed doing during the COVID-19 pandemic, this later resulted in numerous open positions as the world has resumed its pre-pandemic function. This has also led to an increase in hybrid roles, which gives more opportunities to employees with farther commutes.

Technical Skills

Azalea came into SCA without very many technical skills and had to learn them on the job because when she entered the archive, there was no infrastructure set up for the technical aspects. She created the metadata standards for the collections and learned about customized and local schemas for the community-based collections.

Although she did not have the experience coming into her position which reflected the needs of the job, she emphasized the importance of at least becoming familiar with these programs beforehand. Specifically, becoming comfortable with digital repositories and metadata standards before entering into the field can set your application apart. Another skill highlighted is the importance of knowing how digital repositories intersect with other content management and discovery systems. Cal State LA uses Alma/Primo, which is the discovery system for the University Library. The skills you need depend on the job you’re applying for, so familiarizing yourself with the systems already being used in the repository you’re hoping to work in can strengthen your application. SCA was recently looking for a project-based digital preservationist and metadata expert to fill a gap in the current team and support the digitization of an existing collection.

While technical skills are important, Azalea believes tech will come and go and that AI needs to be thought of more critically. She doesn’t feel as though AI can remove the people aspect from librarianship and archivism and that the process itself connects the human experience. However, she does argue that AI exacerbates the loneliness aspect of the digital world. What she emphasizes the most is that soft skills are incredibly important for archival and librarian careers. Overall, she emphasized how people skills and communication are paramount above technical skills (which can always be learned).

Training

Azalea reflected that the main technical skills and software she had to learn included different types of digital repositories to provide access to archival collections. She started with CONTENTdm, then DSpace, and now uses Luna at Cal State LA. Finding an internship which familiarizes you with digital asset management software can help someone who is interested in entering into University Archives or Special Collections. Archival description and preservation metadata were also flagged as being important skills to have as someone entering into the field. Although she listed a number of specialized skills, as the Head of SCA, Azalea insisted on the importance of being well-rounded. She highlighted the importance of developing social skills and having the ability to do outreach to donors and other peers in the field as being crucial for professional development.

Labor Trends

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the field of Archivists, Curators and Museum Workers is increasing by 6% with an average of 4,800 openings for archivists, curators and museum workers each year (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2025). Interestingly, our conversation with Azalea did not necessarily reflect this projected growth. She mentioned how most positions are temporary or contract-based, and with budget cuts happening across university systems, these opportunities are occurring less frequently. Since Azalea is the only full-time faculty employed in the Special Collections, her workload is incredibly large. This is a trend she sees happening across other universities as budget cuts are unable to keep archivists who handle processing and maintenance employed. For Cal State LA specifically, their mission statement which focuses primarily on marginalized communities, has put the SCA at risk of losing funding related to federal DEI budget cuts and grants.

The implementation of AI within the library system at Cal State LA has created new expectations for librarians to answer and address the use of these new technologies. This has been additional labor for librarians, and something they weren’t consulted about before its implementation began. The library named itself the “AI Leader” on campus, however the librarians were given no instruction on how to fulfill that new title. This new role of “AI Leader” on campus has placed the University librarians in a sometimes difficult mediating position between unhappy professors who are concerned about AI usage for assignments and the University which has given all students free access to AI tools and encourages its use. This dynamic can be described as invisible labor which the librarians have to take on in addition to their normal workload. As a result, the librarians, on top of their regular tasks, have now been asked to learn these skills, lead workshops on campus and create regulations and guidelines for students to use.
