Azalea Camacho, MLIS
Head of Special Collections and Archives (SCA) at Cal State LA

Azalea is currently the only full-time faculty in Special Collections but oversees contracted, temporary archivists and student archivists.
  • San Jose State University: MLIS with a specialization in Archival Studies
  • Early experience includes a digitization project for JSTOR through UCLA at the Southern Regional Library.
  • In 2014, she began her journey at Cal State LA as a University Librarian Archivist, later becoming a Special Collections Librarian and eventually assuming the Head position in Special Collections

Cyan: Oh, yeah. The first question is: what technical skills are most essential for an archivist in your position today?

Azalea: Okay, technical skills. In my current role, I don’t do that much technical work. When I first started at Cal State LA, I did a lot more technical work—starting a digitization project and figuring out how to digitize materials in-house. It was just me and one student worker, so I had to learn a lot of technical skills on the job.

Some of the main technical areas are still relevant now, though things have changed with AI. The main technical skills and software I had to learn included different types of digital repositories to provide access to archival collections. I started with CONTENTdm, then DSpace, and now we use Luna at Cal State LA.

I set up some of the technical aspects and the metadata standards for our collections. There are standard metadata schemas like Dublin Core, but sometimes you need local fields depending on the collections. Since we work with a lot of community-based collections, some fields are customized and more local. I learned a lot of that on the job.

So I’d say technical skills include learning about digital repositories and metadata. Also, how those repositories intersect with other content management and discovery systems. At Cal State LA we use Alma/Primo, which is the discovery system for the university library. For better discovery, you want digital collections to be discoverable there too, since that is what students and the campus community mainly use.

We have different systems for different things, like one for video and oral histories. And now AI makes some metadata tasks easier, but it is still problematic. Vendors will try to sell systems with AI functionality. I tested one with a sample of our collections—photographs and documents. AI generated metadata, and it could be useful for pulling text and creating summaries, but you still have to check it. You cannot just use it as-is.

Especially with community archives, you want to make sure things are described the way the community wants to be described. There still needs to be someone involved in the process.

We also use AI for transcripts. For oral histories, we use Otter AI, and then student assistants review it and listen to the audio to make sure it captures it accurately. AI is useful, but there still needs to be a human presence, especially with community archives.

I think you need to be well-rounded. If you understand archival description, you can understand metadata standards for digitization and access. There is also a more technical preservation side—preservation metadata—that I did not do as much of. That is another area. Also website preservation and related work.

I hope that answers the question.

Also understanding how databases connect: if we put digital collections on Luna, how do we crosswalk that into Alma/Primo so it’s centralized and discoverable through the main system campus uses.

Araceli: I wanted to ask about funding and how it impacts these projects. Two parts: how do funding or institutional constraints affect digitization and digital projects? And do you think those roles are increasingly contract-based, or has the job market shifted because of digitization needs?

Azalea: I’ll try to answer. Yes, archival work has often been project-based, so there’s always been temporary, project-based work. That is common even beyond current resource constraints.

For digitization and digital skills, there is project work like everything else, but I also think there is permanency. In institutions with funds, there will likely be more effort to hire people with those skills. In libraries, you see roles like “AI librarian” or “AI research librarian,” and I could see similar roles in archives, like digital initiatives archivist.

There will also be a need for verifying information, because AI hallucinates. Even if AI tools generate metadata, someone still needs to review it. With photographs, AI will not recognize many people (maybe it recognizes a few prominent figures, but not others). With community archives, it can be especially problematic—for example, with protest materials and how people are named or not named. There still needs to be a human review process.

Azalea: I also see people using AI for searching collections, and it can create false information. I had a request where someone claimed we had their grandfather’s collection and an exhibit from the 1970s, based on AI output. I had never heard of it, and I’ve been at Cal State LA for 12 years. They also said their relative was a professor in the 1920s or 1930s, but Cal State LA did not start until 1947. So AI can mislead people. You still need critical thinking and a human component.

Azalea: I don’t know if it has affected special collections directly yet. Students sometimes treat me like I’m AI when they ask questions—they expect immediate, exact responses. They want me to outline everything, analyze primary sources for them, and so on. So I have seen it impact expectations.

I also see people using it to search, and it hallucinates information (like the example I mentioned). But I haven’t seen it affect my archival processes much yet.

It has affected the library more. The library dean wants the library to be the AI leader on campus, and there is talk of reorganization. There are also discussions at the Academic Senate. I’m chair of the Education Policy Committee, so this is more about education and universities: some professors are okay with AI, others are completely against it. Students want the syllabus to state the professor’s policy on AI—whether it is allowed, and how to use it appropriately. There are discussions about an AI guide for graduate students coming out.

It has not trickled down to special collections in a direct way yet, but it has in the library and across higher education. I might see effects later, depending on what “AI leader” ends up meaning for archives.

Tania: Would you be able to speak on other librarians’ experiences with invisible labor in regards to AI?

Azalea: I think the invisible labor comes with the fact that they have to still do their jobs—like one of the librarians is ethnic studies, so that’s her subject area and we have a liaison model so she’s a liaison to that…so that’s additional labor that she has to do to work on this AI group. But she wants to because there’s like ethical…critical thinking with AI and she wants students to learn that—I think we all do; I do too. so i’ve been in discussion with her and other librarians about it.

So i think it’s like a heavy lift because we’re limited in resources at CSULA, and in her case in particular—she’s the chair of the faculty policy committee—there’s a lot of additional labor that happens when you’re a chair of a committee, and then you want to be involved because you wanna share aspects of the library, because we’re not teaching faculty but we are faculty. and a lot of the time librarians are not given release time for being a chair, so there’s a lot of invisible labor that the university does not acknowledge or understand about librarian work. Araceli: And for our last question, what changes would you like to see in the field moving forward? What changes and improvements would you like to see?

Azalea: I have a very specific approach to archives and how I do my work. I think technology is gonna come and go. A lot of people are freaking out about AI, and I think they should. We should think critically about it and the use of it and how people respond to it. I think that people are already lonely and can’t communicate with each other on a personal level and that AI kinda exacerbates that. I think with archives, while you can digitize it and AI or whatever can describe it, I think the beauty of archives and primary sources is that you have to connect the dots and make your own conclusions of how these documents connect and the stories that they’re telling and the community they came from—so it’s all about people.

So I take, like, a person-centered approach to archival theory and practice, and it’s like the way we process collections is centered on people, the people who process the collections, who create the collections, who use the collections… I don't think AI will ever take away that human component away from the experience of looking at a physical document. I think it makes things easier for people, but I think there’s something about looking at a physical object from the past and the human experience and I think that those skills—they call them soft skills—are what you need to move yourself up.

And I know we’re talking a lot about technical skills, but you need the soft skills. because you can learn the technical skills, but you need to learn how to navigate with people, work with people, and build yourself up and move where you wanna go. and I think that’s the biggest skill that people should work on is their people skills and communication.

Araceli: Cool, that’s great!

Azalea: If you can communicate well and you can navigate, I think those are the real skills that will propel yourself up. Because you can learn the technical stuff.
