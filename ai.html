California State University, Los Angeles Special Collections Archive

AI Guidelines

LIS Org: California State University, Los Angeles: Special Collections Archive Existing Guidelines: None Ecological Perspective: Needs guidance for ethical/responsible use

✦ ✦ ✦

Generative AI has the potential to revamp and serve as a catalyst to significantly transform operations and services in information institutions such as libraries, archives, and museums. These technologies are increasingly capable of assisting with a variety of tasks that traditionally require extensive human labor. For example, AI tools can: Generate descriptions Create keywords for cataloging Process audio visual material Virtual reference assistant Automation of metadata for digital collections. Currently, there is no expectation AI will be implemented within the Special Collections Archive (SCA) anytime soon. However, that does not mean that AI will not be considered as a tool in the future. The SCA preserves a wide range of primary research materials related to the history of East Los Angeles, including community histories, oral histories, photographs, and materials related to civil rights movements. Although AI is not currently used, several collections could benefit from AI assisted process: The Voces del Teatro Oral History Archive, which currently is unprocessed, can benefit from AI assisted transcription as a speech-to-text tool to transcribe oral histories and interviews. For the Dymally (Mervyn M.) Papers,which is currently going through processing for the digital archive, AI digitization could help process large collections of images and archival materials as they are digitized and uploaded to repositories. Within the CSULA ecosystem, several stakeholders influence how AI may be implemented in the archival and library environment. Primary stakeholders include the Dean and Chancellor of the California State University system. They have the power to approve or veto policies, allocate funding, and set priorities for technology adoption. The CSU Chancellor has signaled an interest in AI implementation by partnering with ChatGPT. Additionally, the Dean of CSULA has expressed a vision of positioning the university as an “AI leader” within the CSU campuses. Encouraging the exploration of AI tools within library and academic spaces. Students and faculty also key stakeholders, as they have vested interest in how AI can affect their academic work. In a survey conducted by grad students at CSULA, the students’ petitioned to have each professor state their AI policy clearly in their syllabi. Library staff are another important stakeholder group as they are responsible for implementing AI related services and are concerned with AI literacy amongst users. Lastly, information technology (IT) staff play a crucial role in developing and maintaining infrastructure required to support AI systems across campus platforms. All of these stakeholders impact how AI can be incorporated in the campus’ library and archive, and impact the ways in which archival materials are processed and preserved. While AI offers promising opportunities, several urgent issues must be addressed: Ensuring the trustworthiness of AI information is critical. AI systems are known to produce “hallucinations,” or fabricated information, which poses a grave concern for libraries and archives who are expected to provide reliable information. Copyright and intellectual property issues remain unsolved. AI training draws from data that can include copyrighted material. Ethical considerations surrounding privacy and sensitive archival materials must be carefully managed. Archival materials can include personal records and community histories that require protection, and unrestricted AI access could risk the exposure of sensitive material. To address these challenges, institutions must prioritize transparency, accountability, and responsible implementation of AI technologies. Human oversight is key and should remain central to any AI assisted workflow to ensure archival descriptions, metadata, and information access remain accurate and culturally respectful. Efforts to mitigate algorithmic bias and promote equity must be included in AI policies. The development of strong AI literacy programs for staff, faculty, and students will be essential to critically evaluate AI information. Responsible AI implementation within archival institutions will require ongoing collaboration, evaluation, and adaptation. By balancing technological innovation with ethical responsibility and professional standards. CSULA Special Collections Archive can explore AI’s potential while continuing to protect and preserve integrity of historical records.
